  • metacognition //ˌmet.ə.kɒɡˈnɪʃ.ən//

    • 🇺🇸 Awareness and understanding of one’s own thought processes.
    • 🇨🇳 元认知，对自身思维过程的认知
    • 📝 Developing metacognition helps students regulate their learning strategies.

  • cognitive dissonance //ˈkɒɡ.nɪ.tɪv ˈdɪs.ə.nəns//

    • 🇺🇸 The mental discomfort experienced by holding conflicting beliefs or values.
    • 🇨🇳 认知失调，因持有矛盾观念而产生的不适
    • 📝 He felt cognitive dissonance when his actions contradicted his moral principles.

  • rumination //ˌruː.mɪˈneɪ.ʃən//

    • 🇺🇸 The act of repetitively thinking about the same problem or negative experience.
    • 🇨🇳 反刍思维，反复思考同一问题或负面经历
    • 📝 Rumination is a common symptom of depression and anxiety disorders.

  • neuroplasticity //ˌnjʊə.rəʊ.plæˈstɪs.ɪ.ti//

    • 🇺🇸 The brain’s ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life.
    • 🇨🇳 神经可塑性，大脑终身重组形成新连接的能力
    • 📝 Therapy can harness neuroplasticity to help patients recover from strokes.

  • resilience //rɪˈzɪl.i.əns//

    • 🇺🇸 The capacity to recover quickly from difficulties; mental toughness.
    • 🇨🇳 心理韧性，从困难中快速恢复的能力
    • 📝 Building resilience is essential for coping with life’s inevitable setbacks.

  • executive function //ɪɡˈzek.jʊ.tɪv ˈfʌŋk.ʃən//

    • 🇺🇸 A set of cognitive processes that regulate goal-directed behavior, such as planning and inhibition.
    • 🇨🇳 执行功能，调节目标导向行为的认知过程
    • 📝 ADHD often impairs executive function, making organization difficult.

  • heuristic //hjʊˈrɪs.tɪk//

    • 🇺🇸 A mental shortcut that allows people to solve problems quickly, though sometimes inaccurately.
    • 🇨🇳 启发式思维，快速解决问题的心智捷径
    • 📝 Availability heuristic leads us to overestimate the likelihood of vivid events.

  • affective //əˈfek.tɪv//

    • 🇺🇸 Relating to moods, feelings, and attitudes.
    • 🇨🇳 情感的，与情绪和态度相关的
    • 📝 Affective disorders like depression affect millions worldwide.

  • schema //ˈskiː.mə//

    • 🇺🇸 A mental framework that organizes and interprets information about the world.
    • 🇨🇳 图式，组织解释世界信息的心理框架
    • 📝 Our schema for a classroom includes desks, chairs, and a teacher.

  • anhedonia //ˌæn.hɪˈdoʊ.ni.ə//

    • 🇺🇸 The inability to feel pleasure in normally pleasurable activities.
    • 🇨🇳 快感缺失，无法从通常愉悦的活动中感到快乐
    • 📝 Anhedonia is a core symptom of major depressive disorder.

📖 Part 3: Deep Reading

The Mind’s Elasticity: How Cognitive Psychology Illuminates Mental Health

For centuries, the human mind was treated as an immovable entity—static, predetermined, and immune to change. Yet the advent of cognitive psychology has dismantled this antiquated notion, revealing that our mental landscapes are not only malleable but also profoundly shaped by thought patterns, beliefs, and environmental cues. At the heart of this revolution lies a simple yet radical premise: what we think influences how we feel and behave. This insight has reshaped mental health treatment, offering hope where once there was only resignation.

Consider the phenomenon of cognitive dissonance, first articulated by Leon Festinger in 1957. When our actions clash with our values, we experience an unsettling tension—a psychological itch that we are desperate to scratch. To relieve this discomfort, we often realign our beliefs to match our behavior, a process that can either foster growth or entrench self-deception. For instance, a person who values health but smokes may convince themselves that ‘stress relief’ outweighs the risks, rather than quitting. The dissonance is resolved, but at the cost of rationalization.

More insidious is rumination, the relentless replaying of negative events or worries. While reflection can be constructive, rumination traps the mind in a loop—much like a broken record—amplifying distress and eroding resilience. Research in neuroplasticity, however, offers a counterpoint: the brain is not fixed. Through practices like mindfulness and cognitive restructuring, individuals can rewire neural pathways, dampening the grip of rumination and boosting emotional regulation. This is the cornerstone of cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), one of the most effective treatments for anxiety and depression.

Yet understanding these mechanisms is only half the battle. Affective states—our moods and emotions—interact with cognitive processes in a bidirectional dance. A depressed mood can bias our schemas (mental frameworks) toward negativity, coloring how we interpret everyday events. Conversely, a positive affect can broaden attention and foster creativity, as posited by Barbara Fredrickson’s broaden-and-build theory. The implications for mental health are clear: nurturing positive emotions is not mere hedonism; it is a strategic investment in psychological resilience.

In the end, the key takeaway from cognitive psychology is empowerment. We are not passive victims of our minds. By understanding heuristics, biases, and the dynamics of attention, we can take the reins—cultivating metacognitive awareness, challenging distorted thinking, and building mental habits that sustain well-being. The mind’s plasticity is both a warning and a promise: we can be trapped by our thoughts, but we can also be liberated by them.
